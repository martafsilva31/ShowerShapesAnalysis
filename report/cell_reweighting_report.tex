\begin{abstract}
Electromagnetic shower shapes (\Reta, \Rphi, \weta) are critical inputs to
photon identification in ATLAS.  Residual data--MC mismodelling of the
calorimeter cell energy deposits degrades the accuracy of these
discriminating variables.  This note presents a cell-level energy
reweighting approach applied to Layer\,2 of the electromagnetic
calorimeter using $Z \to \ell\ell\gamma$ final-state-radiation events from
ATLAS Run\,3 data (2024) and MC23e simulation.

Two correction methods are studied.  Method~1 (M1, flat shift) adjusts
each cell energy by the mean data--MC fraction difference.  Method~2
(M2, shift+stretch) additionally matches the variance by applying a
linear transformation derived from Francisco Almeida's
\texttt{photoncellbasedrw} framework.  Both methods are evaluated across
13~non-crack $\abseta$ bins, the combined $Z \to \ell\ell\gamma$ channel,
three photon conversion scenarios (unconverted, converted, inclusive),
and four pipeline variants covering $\abseta$-only and two-dimensional
($\abseta$, $\pt$) correction binning with loose and tight isolation.

An initial implementation of M2 suffered from catastrophic instabilities
in specific $\abseta$ bins, where divergent variance ratios in peripheral
cells produced $\chisq$ values up to $90\times$ worse than uncorrected MC.
Three targeted fixes --- histogram-based $\sigma$ estimation via
\texttt{GetRMS()}, cell-dependent histogram ranges acting as natural
regularisation, and removal of a minimum-fraction fallback --- completely
eliminated these instabilities.  The stabilised M2 now outperforms M1 for
all three shower shape variables, with average $\chisq$ improvements of
17\% (\Reta), 45\% (\Rphi), and 29\% (\weta) relative to M1.  M2 nearly
matches the empirical ATLAS fudge factors, achieving identical $\chisq$ for
\Reta\ and \weta\ and within 20\% for \Rphi.

A cross-source comparison with Francisco Almeida's
\texttt{photoncellbasedrw} Run\,3 output confirms equivalent correction
quality, with small residual $\chisq$ differences attributable to
different histogram axis ranges used in the two implementations.
M2 with cell-dependent histogram ranges is the recommended cell-level
correction method.  The \texttt{eta\_pt\_loose} variant (14~$\abseta$
$\times$ 6~$\pt$ bins, loose isolation) achieves the best correction
quality with $\langle\chisq\rangle = 0.0003$ for all three shower shape
variables in the inclusive scenario.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%% ====================================================================

Photon identification in ATLAS relies on shower shape variables computed
from the energy deposits in the electromagnetic calorimeter.  In
particular, \Reta, \Rphi, and \weta\ are built from the Layer\,2
cell energies in a $7 \times 11$ ($\eta \times \phi$) cluster and are
among the most discriminating variables for separating prompt photons
from hadronic fakes.

Residual mismodelling of these variables in Monte Carlo simulation is
addressed by the ATLAS ``fudge factor'' framework, which applies
empirical corrections to the branch-level shower shape values.  An
alternative approach, pioneered by Francisco Almeida in Run\,2
\cite{francisco}, operates at the cell-energy level: the individual cell
energies are corrected before recomputing the shower shapes, thereby
preserving the physical correlations between cells.

This note documents the Run\,3 implementation of the cell-level
reweighting approach.  Two methods are studied:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Method~1 (M1)}: a flat shift per cell, matching the
        population mean of the cell energy fraction.
  \item \textbf{Method~2 (M2)}: a shift+stretch transformation, matching
        both the mean and the variance, following Francisco Almeida's
        \texttt{photoncellbasedrw} method \cite{francisco}.
\end{itemize}
The analysis uses $Z \to \ell\ell\gamma$ events selected from ATLAS 2024
data and MC23e simulation, processed through the NTupleMaker framework.

An earlier version of this pipeline found M2 to be unstable, with
catastrophic failures in certain $\abseta$ bins traced to divergent
variance ratios in peripheral cells.  This note describes the three code
improvements that eliminated those instabilities and presents the
resulting performance, where M2 is now the recommended cell-level
correction method.

\section{Data and MC Samples}
\label{sec:samples}

The analysis uses ATLAS data collected in 2024 and the corresponding
MC23e simulation campaign.  Two channels are considered:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $Z \to ee\gamma$: data from DAOD\_EGAM3 (\texttt{egam3.root}),
        MC sample DSID~700770.
  \item $Z \to \mu\mu\gamma$: data from DAOD\_EGAM4 (\texttt{egam4.root}),
        MC sample DSID~700771.
\end{itemize}
The combined channel ($Z \to \ell\ell\gamma$) merges both data and MC
samples.  Ntuples are produced by the NTupleMaker package running in
Athena~25.0.40.

MC events are weighted by $w = w_{\text{MC}} \times w_\mu \times \sigma$,
where $w_{\text{MC}}$ is the generator weight, $w_\mu$ the pile-up
reweighting factor, and $\sigma$ the cross-section weight.

Events are selected in two stages: a preselection applied at the
NTupleMaker level during ntuple production, and an offline selection

The NTupleMaker applies Good Runs List (GRL) filtering, trigger

required to have a complete $7 \times 11$ Layer\,2 cell cluster

        no ID requirement, with loose isolation.  Event counts are the
        sum of the unconverted and converted samples.
\end{itemize}

\section{Shower Shape Variables}
\label{sec:shower-shapes}
%% ====================================================================

The three shower shape variables are computed from the $7 \times 11$
Layer\,2 cell energy cluster centred on the cell with the highest energy
(index~38 in the row-major ordering).  The cell granularity is
$\Delta\eta = 0.025$ and $\Delta\phi \approx 0.0245$.

\begin{description}
  \item[\Reta] Ratio of the energy in a $3 \times 7$ ($\eta \times \phi$)
    window to the energy in the full $7 \times 7$ window:
    \begin{equation}
      \Reta = \frac{E_{3 \times 7}}{E_{7 \times 7}} \,.
    \end{equation}

  \item[\Rphi] Ratio of the energy in a $3 \times 3$ window to the energy
    in a $3 \times 7$ window:
    \begin{equation}
      \Rphi = \frac{E_{3 \times 3}}{E_{3 \times 7}} \,.
    \end{equation}

  \item[\weta] Energy-weighted $\eta$ width in a $3 \times 5$ window
    centred on the hottest cell:
    \begin{equation}
      \weta = \sqrt{\frac{\sum_i E_i \, (\eta_i - \bar\eta)^2}{\sum_i E_i}}
      \,,\qquad
      \bar\eta = \frac{\sum_i E_i \, \eta_i}{\sum_i E_i} \,,
    \end{equation}
    where the sum runs over the $3 \times 5$ cells.  A position-dependent
    correction is applied following the ATLAS convention.
\end{description}

The energy windows $E_{n \times m}$ are computed from the cell energies
using the cell $\eta$ and $\phi$ positions to identify which cells fall
within the window, following the implementation in \texttt{config.h}.

\paragraph{Validation.}
The cell-computed shower shapes are validated against the values stored
in the ntuple branches (which are the official ATLAS reconstruction
values).  Figure~\ref{fig:computed-vs-stored} shows excellent agreement,
confirming that the cell-level computation faithfully reproduces the
standard shower shapes.

\section{Correction Methods}
\label{sec:methods}
%% ====================================================================

Both correction methods operate on the cell energy fractions
$f_k = E_k / \Etot$, where $E_k$ is the energy of cell~$k$ and
$\Etot = \sum_{k=1}^{77} E_k$ is the total cluster energy.
Corrections are derived independently for each of 14~$\abseta$ bins
(Section~\ref{sec:selection}).

\subsection{Method~1: flat shift}
\label{sec:m1}

For each cell~$k$ and $\abseta$ bin, the correction is defined as the
difference in population-mean fractions between data and MC:
\begin{equation}
  \Delta_k = \langle f_{\text{data}} \rangle_k
           - \langle f_{\text{MC}} \rangle_k \,.
  \label{eq:m1-delta}
\end{equation}
The corrected cell energy is then
\begin{equation}
  E'_k = E_k + \Delta_k \times \Etot \,.
  \label{eq:m1-apply}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Energy conservation.}
Since $\sum_k \langle f_{\text{data}} \rangle_k = \sum_k \langle
f_{\text{MC}} \rangle_k = 1$, the sum of corrections vanishes:
$\sum_k \Delta_k = 0$.  Therefore $\sum_k E'_k = \Etot$ exactly, with
no need for renormalisation.

M1 is simple and robust: it corrects the mean cell profile but does not
modify the variance of the cell fraction distribution.

\subsection{Method~2: shift+stretch (Francisco's method)}
\label{sec:m2}

Method~2 additionally matches the standard deviation of the cell
fraction distribution.  For each cell~$k$, define:
\begin{align}
  \text{stretch}_k &= \frac{\sigma_{\text{data},k}}{\sigma_{\text{MC},k}}
  \,,\label{eq:m2-stretch}\\
  \text{shift}_k &= \mu_{\text{data},k}
                   - \text{stretch}_k \times \mu_{\text{MC},k}
  \,,\label{eq:m2-shift}
\end{align}
where $\mu$ and $\sigma$ denote the population mean and standard
deviation of the cell fraction $f_k$ in data and MC respectively.
The corrected cell energy is
\begin{equation}
  E'_k = \Etot \times \text{shift}_k
       + \text{stretch}_k \times E_k \,.
  \label{eq:m2-apply}
\end{equation}

This is a linear transformation of the cell energy.  At the population
level:
\begin{itemize}
  \item The mean is matched:
    $\langle E'_k / \Etot \rangle = \text{stretch}_k \cdot \mu_{\text{MC},k}
    + \text{shift}_k = \mu_{\text{data},k}$.
  \item The standard deviation is matched:
    $\sigma'_k = \text{stretch}_k \cdot \sigma_{\text{MC},k}
    = \sigma_{\text{data},k}$.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Energy conservation.}
Unlike M1, M2 does not automatically conserve total energy.  A
rescaling step is applied after correction:
\begin{equation}
  E'_k \leftarrow E'_k \times \frac{\Etot}{\sum_{j} E'_j} \,.
  \label{eq:m2-rescale}
\end{equation}

This method corresponds to Francisco Almeida's
\texttt{photoncellbasedrw} approach \cite{francisco}, adapted for
Run\,3 data.


\subsubsection{Variance estimation: histogram-based $\sigma$ via \texttt{GetRMS()}}
\label{sec:m2-getrms}

The standard deviation $\sigma_k$ that enters Eq.~\eqref{eq:m2-stretch}
must be estimated from finite samples.  The natural approach is the
textbook running-variance formula:
\begin{equation}
  \hat\sigma_k^2 = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N} f_{k,i}^{\,2}
                   \;-\; \Bigl(\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N} f_{k,i}\Bigr)^{\!\!2}
  \,,
  \label{eq:manual-variance}
\end{equation}
where $f_{k,i}$ is the energy fraction of cell~$k$ in event~$i$.  This
formula is algebraically correct but \textbf{numerically unstable} when
$\sigma_k$ is small relative to $\mu_k$.  For the central cell
(index~38), which carries $\mu_{38} \approx 0.07$--$0.10$ of the cluster
energy and has $\sigma_{38} \approx 0.01$--$0.02$, the formula is safe.
However, for peripheral cells that carry $\mu_k < 10^{-3}$ of the total
energy, both $\sigma_{\text{data},k}$ and $\sigma_{\text{MC},k}$ are of
order $10^{-4}$, and the subtraction
$\langle f^2 \rangle - \langle f \rangle^2$ involves two nearly equal
numbers of order $10^{-6}$--$10^{-8}$.  In floating-point arithmetic,
this is a classic \textbf{catastrophic cancellation}: the result can be
dominated by accumulated rounding errors, particularly when $N$ is large
(here $N \sim 200\,\text{k}$ data events and $\sim 2.8\,\text{M}$ MC
events).  Even a tiny relative error in the accumulated sum $\sum f_{k,i}^2$
can flip the sign of the difference or inflate $\hat\sigma$ by factors
of 2--10, which then propagates into the stretch factor
$\text{stretch}_k = \sigma_{\text{data},k} / \sigma_{\text{MC},k}$
as a multiplicative error.

The initial implementation of this pipeline used the running-variance
formula of Eq.~\eqref{eq:manual-variance}, which was identified as one
of the root causes of the M2 instability (see
Section~\ref{sec:disc-m2-stabilisation}).

The fixed implementation replaces the manual calculation with a
\textbf{per-cell \texttt{TH1D} histogram approach}.  For each cell~$k$
and $\abseta$ bin, a one-dimensional histogram
\texttt{h\_frac\_d[e][k]} (data) and \texttt{h\_frac\_m[e][k]} (MC) is
booked during initialisation, for a total of
$77 \times 14 = 1\,078$ histograms per sample (data and MC).  During
the event loop, the cell fraction $f_k = E_k / \Etot$ is filled into
the corresponding histogram.  After all events are processed, the
standard deviation is extracted as:
\begin{equation}
  \sigma_{\text{data},k} = \texttt{h\_frac\_d[e][k]->GetRMS()} \,,
  \qquad
  \sigma_{\text{MC},k} = \texttt{h\_frac\_m[e][k]->GetRMS()} \,.
  \label{eq:getrms}
\end{equation}
ROOT's \texttt{GetRMS()} computes the sample standard deviation from
the histogram contents using a numerically stable algorithm that
operates on the binned distribution, avoiding the catastrophic
cancellation inherent in Eq.~\eqref{eq:manual-variance}.

A second critical advantage of the histogram approach is that the
\textbf{histogram range acts as an implicit outlier filter}: any fraction
value $f_k$ falling outside the histogram axis range $[f_{\min},
f_{\max}]$ enters the overflow bin and does \emph{not} contribute to
\texttt{GetRMS()}.  This provides a natural form of regularisation
without requiring explicit outlier-removal logic.  The specific choice
of histogram ranges is discussed in the next section.


\subsubsection{Cell-dependent histogram ranges}
\label{sec:m2-cellranges}

The $7 \times 11$ cluster contains cells with vastly different energy
fractions: the central cell (index~38, directly under the shower core)
carries $\sim$7--10\% of the total cluster energy, while the outermost
cells carry $< 0.01\%$.  Using a single, wide histogram range
$[f_{\min}, f_{\max}]$ for all 77~cells is suboptimal: a range wide
enough to capture the central cell's full distribution (e.g.\
$[-1, 2]$ or $[-0.1, 1.0]$) would spread the peripheral cell's
narrow distribution over very few bins, degrading the \texttt{GetRMS()}
resolution, or --- conversely --- would include extreme stochastic
outliers that inflate the measured standard deviation.

The pipeline therefore implements two range configurations:

\paragraph{(a) Wide uniform range $[-1, 2]$.}
All 77~cells use the same range $[-1, 2]$ with 100~bins (bin width $=
0.03$).  This makes no assumptions about cell energetics but provides
poor resolution for peripheral cells whose entire distribution fits
within a single bin.  Stochastic outlier fractions (e.g.\ negative
energies from noise, or fractions $> 1$ from extreme fluctuations) are
fully included in the RMS.

\paragraph{(b) Cell-dependent ranges (recommended).}
The histogram range is adapted to each cell's expected energy fraction
magnitude, following the ranges used in Francisco Almeida's
\texttt{photoncellbasedrw} framework.  The 77~cells are classified into
four categories based on their position relative to the shower core:

\begin{table}[htbp]
\centering

  Cell indices are 0-based in a row-major $7 \times 11$
  ($\eta \times \phi$) layout, where cell~38 is the central
  (hottest) cell.  All histograms use 100 bins within their respective
  ranges.}
\label{tab:cell-ranges}
\begin{tabular}{llcc}
\toprule
Cell category & Indices (0-based) & Range & Expected $\langle f_k \rangle$ \\
\midrule
Central (shower core)
  & 38
  & $[-0.1, 1.0]$

  & $\sim 0.02$--$0.05$ \\
Diagonal neighbours (next ring)
  & 26, 28, 48, 50
  & $[-0.1, 0.3]$
  & $\sim 0.005$--$0.02$ \\
Peripheral (60 remaining cells)
  & all others
  & $[-0.1, 0.2]$
  & $< 0.005$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}

The cell-dependent ranges serve three purposes:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Resolution}: with 100 bins per histogram, the bin width
        for peripheral cells is $0.003$ (range $[-0.1, 0.2]$) rather
        than $0.03$ (wide range $[-1, 2]$).  This $10\times$ finer
        binning resolves the narrow peripheral distributions and yields
        a more precise \texttt{GetRMS()}.
  \item \textbf{Natural regularisation}: fraction values outside the
        histogram range are clipped into the overflow bin and excluded
        from \texttt{GetRMS()}.  For peripheral cells, whose physical
        fraction is $O(10^{-4})$--$O(10^{-3})$, the upper cutoff at
        $0.2$ removes extreme stochastic tails (e.g.\ from noise
        fluctuations or pile-up) that would inflate $\sigma$ and produce
        pathologically large stretch factors.  This implicit clipping
        replaces the explicit regularisation strategies (Winsorisation,
        peripheral-cell exclusion) that were previously considered.
  \item \textbf{Matching Francisco's implementation}: the ranges in
        Table~\ref{tab:cell-ranges} match those used in the
        \texttt{photoncellbasedrw} framework, ensuring that the M2
        corrections derived by our pipeline are directly comparable to
        Francisco's results.
\end{enumerate}

The effect of the choice of range configuration on the correction
quality is quantified in Section~\ref{sec:disc-wide-vs-cellrange}.
The cell-dependent configuration is used as the primary result
throughout this note.


\subsubsection{Removal of the minimum-fraction fallback}
\label{sec:m2-no-fallback}

The initial implementation of M2 included a safeguard against
pathological stretch factors: cells whose mean fraction
$\mu_{\text{MC},k}$ fell below a threshold
$\texttt{kMinFracForM2} = 0.002$ were excluded from the M2 correction
and instead received only the M1 flat shift.  The rationale was that
peripheral cells with negligible energy carry no useful variance
information, and the M2 stretch factor would be dominated by noise.

This fallback was removed for two reasons:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{The histogram-based $\sigma$ with cell-dependent ranges
        already prevents pathological stretches.}  The implicit
        outlier clipping described in
        Section~\ref{sec:m2-cellranges} ensures that the measured
        $\sigma_{\text{data},k}$ and $\sigma_{\text{MC},k}$ for
        peripheral cells reflect only the physically relevant
        fraction variations, not stochastic noise tails.  The
        resulting stretch factors stay in a well-behaved range without
        any explicit cap or fallback.

  \item \textbf{The fallback created an inconsistent correction
        mixture.}  With the fallback active, each event received a
        mixture of M1 corrections (for cells with $\mu_k < 0.002$)
        and M2 corrections (for cells with $\mu_k \geq 0.002$).
        This was physically inconsistent: the M2 corrections to the
        central cells assumed that \emph{all} cells would be
        shift+stretch-corrected (since the energy conservation
        rescaling~\eqref{eq:m2-rescale} acts on the entire cluster),
        but the peripheral cells received a different transformation.
        The result was a subtle energy imbalance that degraded the
        overall correction quality.  Removing the fallback ensures that
        all 77~cells are corrected with the same M2 linear
        transformation, and the energy conservation rescaling operates
        consistently.
\end{enumerate}

In the current implementation, the only safeguard against division by
zero in Eq.~\eqref{eq:m2-stretch} is a minimum-$\sigma$ threshold:
\begin{equation}
  \text{stretch}_k =
  \begin{cases}
    \sigma_{\text{data},k} / \sigma_{\text{MC},k}
      & \text{if } \sigma_{\text{MC},k} > 10^{-12} \\
    1.0 & \text{otherwise}
  \end{cases}
  \,.
  \label{eq:stretch-safeguard}
\end{equation}
The threshold $10^{-12}$ is far below any physically meaningful variance
and serves only to prevent floating-point division by zero.  In practice
it is never triggered for cells within the histogram range, since even
the most peripheral cells have $\sigma_{\text{MC}} \sim 10^{-4}$--$10^{-3}$.


\subsection{Pipeline variants: binning and isolation}
\label{sec:variants}

The correction pipeline is evaluated in four configurations, referred to
as \emph{variants}, that differ in the correction binning and the photon
isolation requirement.

\begin{table}[htbp]
\centering
\caption{Pipeline variants for the Layer\,2 cell-energy reweighting study.
  All variants use $Z \to \ell\ell\gamma$ (\texttt{llgamma} channel) and
  the three conversion scenarios (unconverted, converted, inclusive).}
\label{tab:variants}
\begin{tabular}{llll}
\toprule
Variant & Binning & Isolation & Correction dimensions \\
\midrule
\texttt{eta\_loose}     & 14 $\abseta$ bins                        & Loose & $14 \times 1 \times 77$ \\
\texttt{eta\_tight}     & 14 $\abseta$ bins                        & Tight & $14 \times 1 \times 77$ \\
\texttt{eta\_pt\_loose} & $14 \times 6$ ($\abseta \times \pt$) bins & Loose & $14 \times 6 \times 77$ \\
\texttt{eta\_pt\_tight} & $14 \times 6$ ($\abseta \times \pt$) bins & Tight & $14 \times 6 \times 77$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}

\paragraph{$\abseta$-only binning.}
Corrections are derived in 14 $\abseta$ bins covering $[0, 2.4]$ with
the calorimeter crack region $[1.37, 1.52]$ excluded.  A single correction
matrix is derived per $\abseta$ bin for all photons regardless of $\pt$.

\paragraph{Two-dimensional ($\abseta$, $\pt$) binning.}
In the extended mode, corrections are derived in $14 \times 6 = 84$
bins, with the six \pt\ boundaries $[10, 15, 20, 25, 30, 40, 1000]$~GeV
supplementing the 14~$\abseta$ bins.  This allows the corrections to
capture any $\pt$ dependence of the data--MC cell-energy mismodelling

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
%% ====================================================================

This section analyses the correction performance in detail, describes
the M2 stabilisation that transformed M2 from an unstable to the
recommended method, compares with Francisco's
\texttt{photoncellbasedrw} reference implementation, and evaluates
the impact of the histogram range configuration.

\subsection{M1 performance}
\label{sec:disc-m1}

Method~1 delivers consistent improvements over uncorrected MC across all
scenario combinations.  For the primary benchmark
($Z \to \ell\ell\gamma$, unconverted):
\begin{itemize}
  \item \Reta: average $\chisq$ reduced from $0.0015$ (uncorrected MC)
        to $0.0006$ (M1), a factor~2.5 improvement.
  \item \weta: reduced from $0.0076$ to $0.0007$, a factor~10.9
        improvement --- the largest gain of any method for any variable.
  \item \Rphi: \textbf{increases} from $0.0007$ to $0.0011$, a
        $1.6\times$ degradation.
\end{itemize}

M1's strength for \Reta\ and \weta\ is expected: both variables are
dominated by the central cells of the $7 \times 11$ cluster, where the
mean cell fraction is large and the shift correction has the most
leverage.

The \Rphi\ degradation is a fundamental limitation of cell-level mean
corrections for ratio observables.
$R_\phi = E(3\!\times\!3) / E(3\!\times\!7)$ is a ratio of two
overlapping energy sums sharing the same $3\!\times\!3$ core.  The M1
shift corrects each cell independently; while the individual cells
improve in mean, the \emph{ratio} of two correlated sums need not
improve --- the correction introduces coherent shifts in both numerator
and denominator that can increase the spread of the ratio distribution.

M1 preserves total cluster energy exactly by construction
($\sum_k \Delta_k = 0$), requiring no rescaling.  This property makes
M1 robust against pathological cell configurations.

However, M1 is \textbf{outperformed by the stabilised M2} for all three
variables (Section~\ref{sec:disc-m2-stabilisation}), and M2 is therefore
the recommended cell-level correction method.

\subsection{M2 stabilisation: from catastrophic failures to best-in-class performance}
\label{sec:disc-m2-stabilisation}

\subsubsection{Previous M2 behaviour (initial implementation)}
\label{sec:disc-m2-old}

The initial implementation of M2 used:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item The running-variance formula~\eqref{eq:manual-variance} to
        compute $\sigma_{\text{data},k}$ and $\sigma_{\text{MC},k}$,
        accumulated as running sums $\sum f_k^2$ and $\sum f_k$ during
        the event loop.
  \item A wide, uniform histogram range $[-1, 2]$ for all cells (used
        only for diagnostic histograms, not for $\sigma$ computation).
  \item A minimum-fraction fallback: cells with
        $\mu_{\text{MC},k} < 0.002$ received M1 instead of M2.
\end{enumerate}

This configuration produced \textbf{catastrophic failures} in specific
$\abseta$ bins.  The worst cases are summarised in
Table~\ref{tab:m2-old-failures}.

\begin{table}[htbp]
\centering
\caption{M2 catastrophic failures in the initial implementation:
  $\abseta$ bins where M2 produced $\chisq$ values far worse than
  uncorrected MC.  These failures are \textbf{no longer present} in
  the stabilised implementation.}
\label{tab:m2-old-failures}
\begin{tabular}{llrrl}
\toprule
$\abseta$ bin & Variable
  & $\chisq^{\text{MC}}$ & $\chisq^{\text{M2,old}}$
  & Ratio \\
\midrule
{[}0.60, 0.80) & $R_\phi$ & 0.0004 & 0.0323 & $81\times$ \\
{[}0.60, 0.80) & $w_{\eta 2}$ & 0.0017 & 0.0481 & $28\times$ \\
{[}1.20, 1.30) & $R_\phi$ & 0.0011 & 0.0641 & $58\times$ \\
{[}1.20, 1.30) & $w_{\eta 2}$ & 0.0016 & 0.0273 & $17\times$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

Inspection of the M2 stretch vectors revealed the root cause:
stretch values were almost exclusively $\geq 1$ and in many cells
\textbf{far larger than physically reasonable}, reaching
$\text{stretch}_k \sim 10$--$16$ in the worst $\abseta$ bins.  This
meant $\sigma_{\text{data},k} \gg \sigma_{\text{MC},k}$: the measured
data cell-energy distributions appeared systematically wider than MC.

The combination of all three issues described in
Sections~\ref{sec:m2-getrms}--\ref{sec:m2-no-fallback} was responsible:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Numerical cancellation} in the running-variance formula
        inflated $\sigma_{\text{data}}$ for peripheral cells where both
        $\sum f^2$ and $(\sum f)^2 / N$ were nearly equal.  Even a
        relative error of $10^{-7}$ in $\sum f_k^2$ (accumulated over
        $\sim$$2.8\,\text{M}$ MC events) could produce a factor-of-2
        error in $\hat\sigma^2$ when $\sigma \sim 10^{-4}$.
  \item \textbf{Wide histogram ranges} (when histograms \emph{were} used
        for diagnostics) included extreme stochastic outliers ---
        negative fractions from noise, fractions $> 1$ from
        mis-reconstruction --- that inflated the tails of the
        distribution and pushed the RMS upward.
  \item \textbf{The minimum-fraction fallback} masked the worst cases by
        reverting to M1 for cells below $\mu < 0.002$, but created an
        inconsistent mixture: 20--40 cells (depending on $\abseta$)
        received M1, while the remaining received M2 with potentially
        large stretches.  The energy conservation rescaling then
        redistributed energy in a way that was neither pure M1 nor
        pure M2, often making both the central and peripheral cells
        worse.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{The three-pronged fix}
\label{sec:disc-m2-fix}

The M2 instabilities were resolved by three simultaneous changes:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Histogram-based $\sigma$ via \texttt{GetRMS()}}
        (Section~\ref{sec:m2-getrms}): replaced the running-variance
        accumulators with per-cell TH1D histograms.  The $\sigma$ is
        now computed from the binned distribution, eliminating the
        catastrophic cancellation in the $\langle f^2 \rangle -
        \langle f \rangle^2$ subtraction.
  \item \textbf{Cell-dependent histogram ranges}
        (Section~\ref{sec:m2-cellranges}):  the histogram range
        for each cell is adapted to its expected fraction magnitude
        (Table~\ref{tab:cell-ranges}).  Extreme outliers beyond the
        range are naturally excluded from the RMS, preventing inflated
        $\sigma$ values in peripheral cells.  This acts as implicit
        regularisation that is physically motivated: fraction values
        outside $[-0.1, 0.2]$ for a peripheral cell are not expected
        from a well-reconstructed photon shower and should not
        influence the correction.
  \item \textbf{Removal of the minimum-fraction fallback}
        (Section~\ref{sec:m2-no-fallback}):  all 77~cells now receive
        the full M2 shift+stretch correction, ensuring a consistent
        transformation across the cluster.
\end{enumerate}

Together, these changes eliminate all three failure mechanisms.  The
fixes are complementary: the histogram approach provides numerical
stability, the cell-dependent ranges provide regularisation, and the
removal of the fallback ensures consistency.

\subsection{Comparison with fudge factors}
\label{sec:disc-fudge}

The ATLAS fudge-factor framework applies an empirical, per-variable
correction to the branch-level shower shape values.  The ``Fudged''
column in the $\chisq$ tables represents the agreement between data
and MC after these corrections.

For unconverted photons in the combined channel, the stabilised M2
achieves:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \Reta: M2 $= 0.0005$, Fudge $= 0.0005$ --- \textbf{identical}.
  \item \Rphi: M2 $= 0.0006$, Fudge $= 0.0005$ --- within 20\%.
  \item \weta: M2 $= 0.0005$, Fudge $= 0.0005$ --- \textbf{identical}.
\end{itemize}

The fudge factors' marginal advantage for \Rphi\ is expected: fudge
factors are tuned \emph{independently per variable} using the full
data--MC comparison for that specific observable, with one free parameter
per $\abseta$ bin per variable.  M2 derives a single set of 77~cell
corrections ($77 \times 2 = 154$ parameters: one shift and one stretch
per cell per $\abseta$ bin) from which all shower shapes are recomputed.
The cell-level approach has fewer effective degrees of freedom to
simultaneously correct three observables built from overlapping cell
windows.

The key advantages of cell-level corrections over fudge factors are:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Physics generality}: any observable built from the
        Layer\,2 cell energies --- including variables not currently
        used in photon ID such as $E_{\text{ratio}}$,
        $f_{\text{side}}$, or new discrimination variables ---
        benefits automatically from the same corrections without
        separate tuning.
  \item \textbf{Correlation preservation}: because the cell energies
        are corrected before the shower shapes are computed, the
        correlations between \Reta, \Rphi, and \weta\ are preserved
        by construction.  Fudge factors, applied independently per
        variable, can distort these correlations.
\end{enumerate}

With M2 now achieving performance virtually identical to fudge factors,
these advantages become decisive: M2 provides the same per-variable
quality while additionally preserving the full multivariate structure.

\subsection{Problematic \texorpdfstring{$\abseta$}{|eta|} regions}
\label{sec:disc-problematic}

Several $\abseta$ regions exhibit elevated $\chisq$ for all methods:

$\chisq = 0.030$ at $\abseta \in [2.2, 2.4)$ for the combined
unconverted channel.  M2 reduces these to
$\chisq \approx 0.0003$--$0.0005$, demonstrating that the cell-fraction
shift+stretch captures the dominant mismodelling even where the detector
geometry differs significantly from the barrel.

\paragraph{Near-crack region ($\abseta \in [1.30, 1.52)$).}
The bins immediately adjacent to the calorimeter crack show elevated
$\chisq$ for all methods.  The $[1.30, 1.37)$ bin has limited data
statistics and residual material effects from the crack region that
affect the shower profile.  All methods produce $\chisq \sim
0.01$--$0.02$ here, which is 5--10$\times$ worse than the barrel
average.  The $[1.52, 1.60)$ bin, immediately after the crack, shows
a similar pattern.  These bins are unlikely to improve without dedicated
treatment.

\paragraph{Converted photons.}
All methods degrade for converted photons relative to unconverted, with
the degradation roughly proportional to $\sqrt{N_{\text{unconv}} /
N_{\text{conv}}}$, suggesting that statistical precision of the
corrections is a limiting factor.  For the combined channel, the
converted dataset has 45\,532 events versus 205\,309 unconverted (a
factor~4.5 fewer), corresponding to a $\sim$$2\times$ increase in
statistical uncertainty on the correction parameters.


\subsection{Isolation requirement}
\label{sec:disc-iso}

Two photon isolation working points are evaluated (see
Table~\ref{tab:iso-yields} in Section~\ref{sec:variants}).  The loose
isolation variant selects $\sim$205\,k data events (unconverted) while
the tight variant selects $\sim$203\,k, a reduction of only $\sim$1\%.
For converted photons the tight selection yields slightly more events
(48\,901 vs.\ 45\,532) due to different signal purities of the two
working points.

The correction quality in Table~\ref{tab:chi2-variant-comparison} shows
that the loose isolation variant produces modestly better M2 performance
for the unconverted scenario: $\chisq(\Reta) = 0.0005$ vs.\ $0.0008$,
$\chisq(\weta) = 0.0005$ vs.\ $0.0008$.  The \Rphi\ variable is
comparable between the two (0.0006 in both cases).  For the converted
and inclusive scenarios the two isolation variants perform similarly.

The slightly lower M2 performance with tight isolation is consistent
with the reduced per-cell statistics for the correction derivation:
fewer events provide a less precise estimate of the cell fraction
distribution, marginally degrading the stretch and shift coefficients.
Given the negligible differences in event yield and the similarity in
M2 performance, \textbf{the loose isolation variant is recommended} as
the default, since it maximises the statistics available for correction
derivation without sacrificing sample purity relevant for the shower
shape study.

\section{Summary}
\label{sec:summary}
%% ====================================================================

This note documents a cell-level energy reweighting pipeline for
correcting photon shower shape variables (\Reta, \Rphi, \weta) in ATLAS
Run\,3 simulation.  Using $Z \to \ell\ell\gamma$ events from 2024 data
and MC23e, corrections are derived and applied across four pipeline
variants (see Table~\ref{tab:variants}) covering $|\eta|$-only and
two-dimensional ($|\eta|$, $\pt$) correction binning with loose and tight
isolation requirements, for three conversion scenarios (unconverted,
converted, inclusive).

Two methods are implemented:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{M1 (flat shift)}: corrects the mean cell energy fraction
        with exact energy conservation.  Reduces the average $\chisq$
        for \Reta\ by a factor~2.5 and for \weta\ by a factor~10.9,
        but degrades \Rphi\ by $1.6\times$ due to the sensitivity of
        $R_\phi = E(3\!\times\!3)/E(3\!\times\!7)$ to coherent mean
        shifts in overlapping windows.
  \item \textbf{M2 (shift+stretch, stabilised)}: additionally matches
        the cell fraction variance using a linear transformation.
        Three targeted improvements --- histogram-based $\sigma$
        estimation via \texttt{GetRMS()}, cell-dependent histogram
        ranges providing natural regularisation, and removal of the
        minimum-fraction fallback --- eliminated the catastrophic
        instabilities ($\chisq$ degradations up to $90\times$) that
        affected the initial implementation.  The stabilised M2 now
        \textbf{outperforms M1 for all three variables}, with
        improvements of 17\% (\Reta), 45\% (\Rphi), and 29\% (\weta).
\end{itemize}

\textbf{M2 with cell-dependent histogram ranges is the recommended
cell-level correction method.}  Its average $\chisq$ nearly matches the
empirical ATLAS fudge factors: identical for \Reta\ and \weta\ (0.0005),
and within 20\% for \Rphi\ (0.0006 vs.\ 0.0005).  Unlike fudge
factors, M2 preserves inter-variable correlations and automatically
improves all observables built from Layer\,2 cell energies.

Among the four pipeline variants, the \textbf{\texttt{eta\_pt\_loose}
variant} --- two-dimensional ($\abseta$, $\pt$) binning with loose
isolation --- achieves the best correction quality for the combined
channel, reaching $\langle\chisq\rangle = 0.0003$ for all three shower
shape variables in the inclusive scenario.  The $\pt$ binning provides
the largest improvement for \Rphi\ (0.0006 $\to$ 0.0004 for unconverted
loose), where the pT dependence of the cell energy profile is not fully
captured by $\abseta$-only corrections.  Loose isolation is preferred
over tight, as it maximises the statistics for correction derivation
with negligible impact on sample purity.

A cross-source comparison with Francisco Almeida's
\texttt{photoncellbasedrw} Run\,3 output confirms equivalent correction
quality.  Small residual $\chisq$ differences are fully attributable to
different shower shape histogram axis ranges (Francisco uses narrower
ranges that exclude the distribution tails, producing systematically
lower $\chisq$ values for the same underlying correction).
